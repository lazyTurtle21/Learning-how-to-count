cards problems

Problem 1. How many three card hands are there in a 52-card deck?
As the order is irrelevant, we get C(52, 3) = 52! / (49! * 3!) = 22100 different 3 card hands.

Problem 2. How many three card hands are there, where each card is a face card (jack, queen, king, or ace)?
Considering the suits of cards, there are 4 * 4 = 16 different face cards, we have to choose 3 out of them, so, we get C(16, 3) = 16! / (13! * 3!) = 560 outcomes.

Problem 3. In how many ways can you pick the suits of the cards that appear in a full house consisting of, say, 3 sixes and 2 queens?
There are 4 different suits, and since we need 3 sixes and 2 queens, there are C(4,3) ways to pick a suit for a queen and C(4,2) to do the same for sixes. Applying the product rule, we get C(4,3) * C(4,2) = 24 ways to pick suits of the cars that appear in a full house.

Problem 4. A deck of 52 cards is dealt to 4 players, 13 to each. What is the number of all different deals?
As the order of cards in hand doesn’t matter, we get C(52, 13) * C(39, 13) * C(26, 13) * C(13, 13) (first we choose 13 cards out of 52 card deck, then – 13 out of 39 card deck and so on) = 52!/(13!)^4 different deals.

dice rolling

Arman rolls two dice. Calculate the total number of different outcomes, and then the number of outcomes in which:
  • Both dice show the same number.
    • There are 6 outcomes when we get the same number at 2 dice because there are 6 variants at 1st rolling and no choice at 2nd one.
  • Their sum is seven or eleven.
    • There are 2 possible outcomes when we get eleven as a sum of numbers on dice: one dice shows 5 and the other - 6 and vice versa. And, there are 6 outcomes when we get 7 as a sum: 1 and 6, 2 and 5, 3 and 4 (and vice versa) as numbers on dice. So, applying the rule of sum, the number of outcomes of getting either 7 or 11 as a sum of dice is 2 + 6 = 8.
  • The two numbers have no common factor greater than 1.
    • Let’s count the outcomes when two numbers have a common factor greater than 1: 2 and 4, 2 and 6, 4 and 6, 3 and 6 and vice versa. In total, we have 8 such outcomes. Subtracting from all possible outcomes and also subtracting pairs (2 and 2, 3 and 3 and so on), we get 36 – 8 - 5 = 23 outcomes.

people permutation

Problem 1. In how many ways n persons can be placed on a bench?
It's an usual permunation, obviously, the aswer is P(n) = n!.

Problem 2. n married couples are at a birthday party. In how many ways can they be seated at a round table so that each husband sits next to his wife?
n couples at a round table can be seated at (n – 1)! ways because if the place is not numbered, then at a fixed seedling, everyone can move to one place without changing the situation, and after that in each married couple we can change the places of husband and wife. By the rule of the product we get (n! * 2^n)/n = (n - 1)! * 2^n ways.

Chess problems

Problem 1. What is the number of bishop rearrangements of a 2 × n board where pieces are permitted to remain in place?
Consider that since any bishop must remain on its original color the white and black pieces can be rearranged independently. Similar to the 1 × n king rearrangements, a natural bijection arises between bishops remaining on their initial square or swapping places and tilings with squares and dominoes. Since each rearrangement can be constructed as a combination of two of these separate Fibonacci rearrangements, the total number must be f(n)^2.

Problem 2. What is the number of knight rearrangements of a 2 × 2n board where pieces are permitted to remain in place?
For each n, note that the squares may be divided into 4 disjoint classes determined by the knight movements. Since a knight initially placed on a square in one of these classes cannot move to a square in another class, we can consider the 4 sets of knights independently. Again, by considering a bijection between rearrangements within a class and Fibonacci tilings, we see that the number of knight rearrangements on a board of dimensions 2 × 2n is equal to f(n)^4.

Permutation of repeated objects

Problem 1. How many different strings can be made from the letters in MISSISSIPPI, using all the letters?
Since there are 1 "M", 4 "I"s, 4 "S"s, 2 "P"s -- totally 11 letters, according to formula of permutations with repeated objects, we can get the final answer: P(4,4,2,1) = 11!/(4! * 4! * 2! * 1!) = 34650 ways to rearrange letters.

Problem 2. How many 4-letter words can be made, using English alphabet?
Since it is not mentioned that the repetions aren't allowed, there are 26 ways to choose the first letter, 26 ways to choose second one and so on. The answer is 26 * 26 * 26 * 26 = 26^4.

Problem 3. A student has three mangos, two papayas, and two kiwi fruits. If the student eats one piece of fruit each day, and only the type of fruit matters, in how many different ways can these fruits be consumed?
Answer: Similar to previous problems, there are total 7 pieces of fruit. The final answer: 7! / ( 3! * 2! * 2! ) = 210.

Distributing Objects into Boxes

How many ways are there to distribute hands of 5 cards to each of four players from the standard deck of 52 cards?
We will use the product rule to solve this problem. To begin, note that the first player can be dealt 5 cards in C(52, 5) ways. The second player can be dealt 5 cards in C(47, 5) ways, because only 47 cards are left. The third player can be dealt 5 cards in C(42, 5) ways. Finally, the fourth player can be dealt 5 cards in C(37, 5) ways. Hence, the total number of ways to deal four players 5 cards each is C(52, 5)C(47, 5)C(42, 5)C(37, 5) = 52! / (5!*5!*5!*5!*32!).

How many ways are there to pack six copies of the same book into four identical boxes, where a box can contain as many as six books?
We will enumerate all ways to pack the books. For each way to pack the books, we will list the number of books in the box with the largest number of books, followed by the numbers of books in each box containing at least one book, in order of decreasing number of books in a box. The ways we can pack the books are: 6; 5 1; 4 2; 4 1 1; 3 3; 3 2 1; 3 1 1 1; 2 2 2; 2 2 1 1. For example, 4, 1, 1 indicates that one box contains four books, a second box contains a single book, and a third box contains a single book (and the fourth box is empty). We conclude that there are nine allowable ways to pack the books, because we have listed them all.
